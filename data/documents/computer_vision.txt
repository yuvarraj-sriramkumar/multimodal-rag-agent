Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) revolutionized computer vision by introducing a learnable, hierarchical feature extraction process that is inherently suited to the spatial structure of images. Unlike fully connected networks, CNNs apply small learnable filters across the spatial dimensions of an input image, producing feature maps that capture local patterns such as edges, textures, and shapes. Successive convolutional layers stack these representations, allowing deeper layers to respond to increasingly abstract concepts — from oriented edges in the first layer to object parts in intermediate layers to full semantic categories in the final layers. Landmark CNN architectures include AlexNet (2012), which demonstrated the power of deep learning on ImageNet; VGGNet, which showed that depth with small filters was key; ResNet, which introduced residual skip connections to train networks over 100 layers deep; and EfficientNet, which scales width, depth, and resolution simultaneously via a compound coefficient.

Image recognition — the task of assigning a label to an entire image — was the proving ground for CNNs, but the field has since expanded to a rich set of vision tasks. Object detection methods like YOLO and Faster R-CNN locate and classify multiple objects within a single forward pass or with a region proposal stage. Semantic segmentation assigns a class label to every pixel, while instance segmentation additionally distinguishes individual object instances. Pose estimation infers the spatial configuration of body keypoints, and optical flow estimation tracks pixel-level motion between frames. All of these tasks now rely predominantly on deep learning approaches that far exceed classical hand-crafted feature methods such as SIFT, HOG, and Haar wavelets.

The advent of vision transformers (ViTs), introduced by Dosovitskiy et al. in 2020, challenged the dominance of CNNs by applying the transformer self-attention mechanism directly to sequences of image patches. ViTs match or surpass CNN performance on large datasets and exhibit superior scalability. Hybrid architectures such as Swin Transformer introduce hierarchical windowed attention to restore the locality and efficiency of convolutions. Multimodal models like CLIP learn joint image-text embeddings through contrastive pre-training on hundreds of millions of image-caption pairs, enabling zero-shot image classification and powering applications in text-to-image generation, visual question answering, and image retrieval.
